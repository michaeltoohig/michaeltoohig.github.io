The 2-1 Split in Port Vila

This is my first post about Vanuatu :vu: and I hope I have more in the future since this country is basically unknown and full of unique languages, stories and cultures. This topic is about an unknown quirk in the culture that leads to this puzzling behavior.

The 2-1 split, as I call it, is simply that given a group of 3 or more Ni-Vanuatu walking together on a road without a footpath or sidewalk available to them will more likely than not, given enough notice to the oncoming vehicle, split themselves so that 1/3 of the group will cross the road to seperate themselves from their walking group and force the driver to pass between the now two groups of pedestrians.

In my opinion this is more dangerous than all members of a walking group remaining on their side of the road because there is less room for a vehicle to safely navigate the remaining space on the road. It feels particularily dangerous when traffic comes from both directions because the pedestrians have now split the already narrow road into essentially 4 lanes which forces someone to be uncomfortably close with another or one driver will have to come to a complete stop to allow the other driver through the middle lane first. The danger created by pedestrians splitting the road is what really highlighted the pattern in my mind and I began to notice it all the time.

I want to emphasis, I’ve seen this behavior in groups walking together up to nearly 10 maybe 12 or 15 members and consistently for the last year I count, assuming the number of people in the group allows for a clean 1/3 split, the smaller break-away group is 1/3 of the total group, I don’t recall a day I saw a 1/2 split away or other fraction, 1/3 of the group splitting away is the unspoken law as far as I can tell. Hence the name was born from the ratio of pedestrians that split away from their walking party to the other side of the road. Really I believe it must be some sort of cultural, social meme that spreads subconscouisly through the population.

My best example of this behavior has to be 3 teenage boys sitting on the road, not even walking on the road, at the bottom of a hill and near a bend in the road (previous details may not be relevant) where one of the boys seeing me at least 50 to 100 meters away decided to stand up and cross the road in front of me to wait for me to pass before returning to his friends on the other side of the road which I saw him do in my rear-view mirror. All the while his two friends did not take any actiona at all and remained seated on the edge of the road as the three of them had been. This example stands out to me since the one boy decided to take, in my opinion, a more risky action by standing and crossing the road rather than allowing me to drift to the left and just give the boys space on the otherwise empty road, now I was forced to split the road with pedestrians on both sides.

Limitations

This behavior may not be universal in Vanuatu, I am biased to the things I see in the capital of Vanuatu. I also didn’t drive in Vanuatu until I moved to the capital so I had no chance to see this behavior on other islands or didn’t recognize it as a pedestrian while I was on those other islands.

Usually this behavior occurs when the following are true:

  • the road has no footpath or sidewalk
  • the road is not a highway or other main road (roads that have support fast drivers)
  • the road is not so wide that crossing the road takes more than several steps to cross
  • the group has sufficient notice to visually identify the approaching vehicle
  • the group must consist of able-bodied children or adults (leaning towards young adults) or contain them in enough numbers to not be out-numbered by the elderly

Less likely the behavior is to occur when:

  • the group is not already sharing the road with another independent group on the opposite side of the road or nearby

As contrived as this list may sound, my brother-in-law confirms that this hits the nail on the head but as an insider to the culture it never really stood out before.

Potential Explanations

Personally, I believe this may be related to some sort of social meme. If I were to try to attribute an evolutionary explanation then perhaps this behavior protects a group as a out-of-control driver can’t kill all pedestrians if some of them are on the other side of the road.

Technology

Because I have to include a section about technology on my website.

I wish to begin recording this behavior with my dash cam and if I didn’t have a family I would make some sort of script using object detection to detect when a person crosses the road in front of, coun the number of people and then save all clips that show this 2-1 split. This is not outside the realm of possibility given some other object detection scripts I’ve made before. Currently, I don’t have video evidence so I can not begin, I’m just not sure it’s necessary where a blog article may be enough to tell the world of this fascinating behavior.

What Next?

I kinda hope to have my dashcam setup and to collect a bunch of examples of this behavior on video. I would probably just make a super-cut of it all then leave it at that for others to wonder why it occurs and how prevalent it actually is compared to my estimates. I would like to know if this behavior is common elsewhere or if someone actually knows a possible reason for it or has noticed this too.

More ambitious yet, I could maybe learn what it would take to do something more academic like publish to some sociology journal or such. But that’s not my field of work and probably best I leave that to someone else as long as I get some credit for bringing attention to it first 😉.
